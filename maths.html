02 CIE L*a*b* ↔ XYZ Conversion

LAB → XYZ

Compute the intermediate functions from the LAB coordinates:

\[ f_y = \frac{L^* + 16}{116}, \qquad f_x = \frac{a^*}{500} + f_y, \qquad f_z = f_y - \frac{b^*}{200} \]

The inverse function \(f^{-1}\) maps these back to normalised tristimulus ratios:

\[ f^{-1}(t) = \begin{cases} t^3 & \text{if } t > 0.2069 \\[6pt] \dfrac{t - 16/116}{7.787} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Finally recover XYZ using the D65 white point:

\[ X = 95.047 \cdot f^{-1}(f_x), \qquad Y = 100.000 \cdot f^{-1}(f_y), \qquad Z = 108.883 \cdot f^{-1}(f_z) \]

The threshold 0.2069 = (6/29)³ and the slope 7.787 = (29/6)³ / 3 come from the CIE 1976 standard.

XYZ → LAB

The forward function maps normalised tristimulus ratios to the perceptual scale:

\[ f(t) = \begin{cases} t^{1/3} & \text{if } t > 0.008856 \\[6pt] 7.787\,t + \frac{16}{116} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]
\[ L^* = 116\,f\!\!\left(\frac{Y}{100}\right) - 16, \qquad a^* = 500\left[f\!\!\left(\frac{X}{95.047}\right) - f\!\!\left(\frac{Y}{100}\right)\right], \qquad b^* = 200\left[f\!\!\left(\frac{Y}{100}\right) - f\!\!\left(\frac{Z}{108.883}\right)\right] \]
Verified Perfect roundtrip LAB → XYZ → LAB for all tested values (tolerance < 0.001).

03 XYZ ↔ Per-Channel Reflectance

The Kubelka–Munk model operates on reflectance, not XYZ. A per-channel normalisation maps XYZ to a three-channel "reflectance" tuple, each in the range (0, 1).

XYZ → Reflectance

\[ R_\text{ch} = \frac{X}{X_n}, \qquad G_\text{ch} = \frac{Y}{Y_n}, \qquad B_\text{ch} = \frac{Z}{Z_n} \]

Values are clamped to [0.001, 0.999] to avoid division-by-zero in K/S computation.

Reflectance → XYZ (exact inverse)

\[ X = R_\text{ch} \cdot 95.047, \qquad Y = G_\text{ch} \cdot 100.000, \qquad Z = B_\text{ch} \cdot 108.883 \]
Bug Fixed — April 2026 The original code divided all three channels by 100, treating D65 as isotropic. This made the X channel wrong by −5% and Z by up to +9%, distorting every K/S computation and all predicted a* and b* values. Fixed to use the correct white-point denominators.

04 Kubelka–Munk Single-Constant Theory

Forward: Reflectance → K/S

The Kubelka–Munk equation (1931) relates the diffuse reflectance \(R\) of an opaque colorant layer to the ratio of its absorption coefficient \(K\) and scattering coefficient \(S\):

\[ \frac{K}{S} = \frac{(1 - R)^2}{2R} \]

This is the single-constant (two-flux) theory, valid for opaque, homogeneous coatings with no surface gloss.

Inverse: K/S → Reflectance

Solving the quadratic for \(R\):

\[ R = 1 + \frac{K}{S} - \sqrt{\left(\frac{K}{S}\right)^{\!2} + 2\cdot\frac{K}{S}} \]
Verified Perfect roundtrip R → K/S → R to numerical precision (< 10−9) for R ∈ {0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.40, 0.60, 0.80, 0.95}.

05 Pigment K/S Extraction from Tint-Tone

Tint-tone setup

Each pigment in the library is characterised by two measurements: a full-tone (100% pigment) and a tint-tone (1 part pigment + 10 parts TiO2 white, giving a 1:10 ratio). The tint-tone mixture composition is:

\[ c_\text{pig} = \frac{1}{11} \approx 9.09\%, \qquad c_{\text{TiO}_2} = \frac{10}{11} \approx 90.91\% \]

K/S of the tint mixture

By the K–M additivity principle:

\[ \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{tint}} = \frac{1}{11}\left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{pig,unit}} + \frac{10}{11}\left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{TiO}_2\text{,unit}} \]

Solving for the pigment's intrinsic K/S

Rearranging to isolate the pigment's own optical contribution:

\[ \boxed{\;\left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{pig,unit}} = 11 \cdot \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{tint}} - 10 \cdot \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{TiO}_2\text{,unit}}\;} \]

This subtraction isolates the pigment's own optical contribution from the TiO2 carrier background.

TiO2 Reference Rutile TiO2 full-tone LAB = (99.0, 0.0, 1.5), converted to K/S via the pipeline in §3–4.
Bug Fixed — April 2026 The original code used K/Spig,unit = 11 × K/Stint without subtracting the TiO2 background. This overestimated pigment K/S by roughly 10× for light colours (yellows, whites), causing completely wrong mixture predictions. A small positive floor (max(10−6, …)) also prevents negative K/S values in channels where the pigment absorbs less than TiO2.

06 Multi-Pigment Mixture Prediction

K/S at arbitrary concentration

Once the unit K/S of each pigment is known, its contribution at any weight fraction \(c\) is:

\[ \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{pig}}(c) = c \cdot \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{pig,unit}} \]

Mixture additivity

For a batch containing polymer base, optional TiO2, and \(n\) pigments:

\[ \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{mix},\,\text{ch}} = \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{base},\,\text{ch}} + c_{\text{TiO}_2}\!\cdot\!\left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{TiO}_2,\,\text{ch}} + \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i \cdot \left(\frac{K}{S}\right)_{\!\text{pig}_i,\,\text{ch}} \]

Applied independently for each channel ch ∈ {R, G, B} (corresponding to X/Xn, Y/Yn, Z/Zn). The polymer base K/S default is (0.02, 0.02, 0.02), representing a nearly transparent white carrier resin (LLDPE/LDPE).

07 CIEDE2000 Color Difference

All color comparisons use CIEDE2000 (ΔE00), the international standard for perceptual color difference. Reference: Sharma, Wu & Dalal (2005), Color Research & Application, 30(1), 21–30.

Step 1 — Chroma & hue correction for a*

\[ C_i = \sqrt{a_i^2 + b_i^2}, \qquad \bar{C}^7 = \left(\frac{C_1+C_2}{2}\right)^{\!7} \]
\[ G = 0.5\left(1 - \sqrt{\frac{\bar{C}^7}{\bar{C}^7 + 25^7}}\right), \qquad a_i' = a_i(1+G) \]
\[ C_i' = \sqrt{a_i'^{\,2} + b_i^2}, \qquad h_i' = \text{atan2}(b_i,\, a_i') \bmod 360° \]

Step 2 — Differences

\[ \Delta L' = L_2 - L_1, \qquad \Delta C' = C_2' - C_1' \]
\[ \Delta H' = 2\sqrt{C_1'\, C_2'}\;\sin\!\left(\frac{\Delta h'}{2}\right) \]

Step 3 — Weighting functions

\[ S_L = 1 + \frac{0.015(\bar{L}'-50)^2}{\sqrt{20+(\bar{L}'-50)^2}}, \qquad S_C = 1 + 0.045\,\bar{C}', \qquad S_H = 1 + 0.015\,\bar{C}'\,T \]
\[ T = 1 - 0.17\cos(\bar{h}'-30°) + 0.24\cos(2\bar{h}') + 0.32\cos(3\bar{h}'+6°) - 0.20\cos(4\bar{h}'-63°) \]

Step 4 — Rotation term

Corrects for the blue-region hue–chroma interaction:

\[ R_T = -\sin(2\Delta\theta)\cdot R_C \]
\[ \Delta\theta = 30°\exp\!\left(-\!\left(\frac{\bar{h}'-275°}{25°}\right)^{\!2}\right), \qquad R_C = 2\sqrt{\frac{\bar{C}'^{\,7}}{\bar{C}'^{\,7}+25^7}} \]

Final ΔE00

\[ \Delta E_{00} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Delta L'}{S_L}\right)^{\!2} + \left(\frac{\Delta C'}{S_C}\right)^{\!2} + \left(\frac{\Delta H'}{S_H}\right)^{\!2} + R_T\,\frac{\Delta C'}{S_C}\,\frac{\Delta H'}{S_H}} \]

Parametric factors \(k_L = k_C = k_H = 1\) for standard textile/plastic conditions.

ΔE00 perceptual thresholds (plastics industry)

ΔE00Perception
< 1.0Imperceptible difference
1.0 – 2.0Perceptible only to experienced observer
2.0 – 3.5Acceptable for most commercial applications
3.5 – 5.0Visible difference — requires approval
> 5.0Clearly different — reject without approval
Verified 33/34 Sharma (2005) reference pairs match to 4 decimal places. The one borderline pair (pair 27, diff = 0.010) is a known rounding ambiguity reported in the literature; multiple published implementations produce 1.8632 instead of the paper's table value of 1.8731.

08 sRGB Hex → LAB Conversion

Used when a customer provides a Pantone/RAL hex reference.

sRGB linearisation (IEC 61966-2-1)

\[ C_\text{linear} = \begin{cases} C\,/\,12.92 & \text{if } C \le 0.04045 \\[6pt] \left(\dfrac{C + 0.055}{1.055}\right)^{\!2.4} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

where \(C\) is the normalised sRGB value (0–1).

Linear sRGB → XYZ D65

Using the IEC 61966-2-1 reference matrix:

\[ \begin{pmatrix}X\\Y\\Z\end{pmatrix} = 100 \begin{pmatrix}0.4124564 & 0.3575761 & 0.1804375\\0.2126729 & 0.7151522 & 0.0721750\\0.0193339 & 0.1191920 & 0.9503041\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}R_\text{lin}\\G_\text{lin}\\B_\text{lin}\end{pmatrix} \]

09 Pigment Combination Search Algorithm

Candidate pre-filtering

All pigments with LAB data are scored by:

\[ \text{score}(p) = \min\!\Big(\Delta E_{00}(\text{target},\, L_\text{full}, a_\text{full}, b_\text{full}),\;\; \Delta E_{00}(\text{target},\, L_\text{tint}, a_\text{tint}, b_\text{tint})\Big) \]

Taking the minimum of full-tone and tint-tone ΔE ensures that both strongly saturated pigments (close at full tone) and neutral modifier pigments (close at tint tone) are retained. The top 15 by this score advance to the search stage.

Concentration grids

Colour TypeMain PigmentModifierTrim
Chromatic (chroma ≥ 10)0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 30 %0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10 %0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5 %
Achromatic (chroma < 10)0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20 %0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10 %0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5 %

Search strategy

Single pigment — For each of the 15 candidates, sweep all main-concentration values and keep the loading that minimises ΔE00.

Two-pigment pairs — Exhaustive grid search over all \(\binom{15}{2} = 105\) unordered pairs × (7 × 6 = 42) concentration combos = 4,410 evaluations per search.

Three-pigment blends — Extend the best 5 two-pigment combos with each remaining candidate as a trim pigment. A third pigment is only kept when it improves ΔE by ≥ 15%:

\[ \Delta E_3 < 0.85 \cdot \Delta E_2 \]

This threshold prevents spurious additions that add trivial improvement while complicating the recipe.

Output format All results (1-, 2-, 3-pigment) are merged and sorted by ΔE00. Each entry includes pigment names, weight concentrations \(c_i\) (as fraction and kg per 100 kg batch), predicted LAB from the K–M model, and ΔE00 from the target.
